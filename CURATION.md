# Full Bleed — Curation Brief

*The source of truth for what gets in, how it's sorted, and why. Last updated June 13, 2026. Supersedes the taxonomy in `project-spec.md` §4.*

## What this is

New, remarkable AI — from roughly the last two months — that a working creative could actually use. What it is, and how to use it.

## The stance

This exists and isn't going anywhere. You don't have to love it, but learning it is how you leverage it. Full Bleed is **education and curation — by creators, for creators.** Not a sales pitch, not a conversion campaign, not doom and not hype. We don't preach to people who've already decided they hate AI; we serve the curious and the already-in.

## Who it's for

Working creatives and the work they're judged on — designers, art/creative directors, photographers, motion designers — plus the decks, ads, client work, and marketing they actually ship. Employed and freelance. And the AI-curious and AI-leads who use it to stay current.

## The bar (every entry clears all of these)

- **New & relevant** — nothing older than ~6 months ever surfaces; the target is ~2. AI moves monthly; stale is worthless.
- **Genuinely impressive or genuinely useful** — a discerning creative thinks "that's actually good," not "ugh, AI slop."
- **Differentiated** — the standout, not the 50th generic version of the same idea.
- **English.**
- **Shows its work** — a real demo, artifact, or result. The date is shown on everything.

## Killed on sight

- AI slop: one-shot ChatGPT outputs, "this poster prompt just dropped," generic wrappers, image dumps.
- Novelty / entertainment with no use — AI short-dramas, games, toys.
- Anything stale, non-English, or framed as "AI replaces ___".
- Money/monetization framing — that's a byproduct of good curation, not a category we cover.

## Axis 1 — Categories (the spine)

Five categories. *What kind of thing it is.* Order matters; Papers is intentionally last.

1. **Tools** — a usable, standalone AI app or product you can pick up and use today.
   *e.g. an app that turns any doc into an editable on-brand deck; a tool that retouches product shots while holding garment detail.*
2. **Automations** — a workflow/pipeline you set up once and it keeps saving you time. Also where real workflows people ship at their jobs live. Comes with a setup walkthrough.
   *e.g. a ComfyUI try-on pipeline that gives full-catalog coverage from one shoot; an agency's brief-to-deck system.*
3. **Models** — a notable new model. **Any major/notable model release always qualifies here — that's the point of this lane.** Do NOT slop-filter a big release for being "generic"; notability *is* the bar. Each entry covers: what the model is · why it matters · what it improved on · its strengths & weaknesses. *(Model comparisons + pricing/best-use intel become a section inside this later.)*
   *e.g. a new virtual try-on or video model; a major multimodal release like Qwen3-Omni.*
4. **Plugins & Skills** — building blocks that wire AI into the stack you already use: plugins, AI skills, MCP servers, API drops. The most technical lane.
   *e.g. a Figma plugin that generates on-brand layouts; a Claude skill that runs a paid-ads audit; a newly shipped MCP server.*
5. **Papers** — notable bleeding-edge research, summarized in plain language. The stay-ahead / education layer: reference points for what's out there and what's gaining traction, **even if you'll never use them yourself.** Judge on whether it's a *notable, worth-knowing* advance in creative-relevant AI (image, video, 3D, design, generation) — **not** on whether a designer could use it tomorrow. Major-lab releases count (e.g. a Meta SAM-class model). Genuinely off-domain work (surveillance, vehicle recognition, pure infrastructure) is still killed.
   *e.g. a new segmentation or multi-view video-diffusion model from a major lab; a consistent-character technique.*

**Tiebreaker — Tools vs Automations (the one fuzzy seam):**
> **Tool** = you use it as-is (a finished product). **Automation** = you assemble/configure it into your workflow (a recipe, and it ships with a setup walkthrough).

So an app that auto-builds decks is a **Tool** (you just use it); a wired-together try-on pipeline is an **Automation**.

## Axis 2 — Disciplines (refinement)

The creative domain a thing serves, applied as **multi-select tags** that ride on top of any category: image · video · motion · design/branding · decks · web · photography · 3D · audio · copy. A photographer narrows to their lane without us inventing new categories.

*On the site this is surfaced as "what you do," not labeled "discipline" — categories are the primary browse axis; disciplines are a lighter refinement on top (see IA notes / UI pass).*

## Every entry

What it is → the link → the key info → how you'd use it (a real walkthrough for Tools/Automations; a sharp "here's how you'd use it" for Models/Papers). Actions: **upvote · save · share** (a shareable "fullbleed link").

## Open and closed — both first-class

The catalog is for working creatives, and most of them work in **commercial tools** (Midjourney, Runway, Sora, Adobe Firefly, Figma AI, ElevenLabs, ChatGPT, Claude). Open-source / open-weight work is welcome, but it is **not** the point — a major closed or paid release (a new Midjourney, a frontier model like Fable 5, a paid design app) is as worthy as any open repo. **Judge the work, never the license.** The intake must reflect the real tool landscape, not just what happens to be downloadable. An open-source-only catalog is a sourcing failure, not the goal.
