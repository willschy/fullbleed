**What it is.** Kodo is an AI design tool that generates structured, editable layouts from a text prompt. You describe what you want — a poster, a menu, a set of slides, a social graphic — and it comes back with a real layered file: editable text, swappable colors, adjustable spacing. The output is a working design, not a flat image you have to recreate by hand.

**Why it matters.** Most AI image tools stop at the picture. Getting from that picture to something you can actually hand off or iterate on means rebuilding the layout in Figma or Illustrator anyway. Kodo skips that translation step. The thing it gives you is already structured, so the first thing you do after generating is edit, not trace.

**How to use it.** Start with a prompt that is specific about format and intent: "a vertical poster for a jazz night, dark background, two headliners, date at the bottom" lands better than something vague. Kodo returns a layered design you can open immediately. From there, click into the text to swap copy, adjust the color palette to match a brand, and nudge spacing until the hierarchy reads the way you want. When it looks right, export. For work that lives inside a bigger project, use Kodo for the initial structure and pull the export into your usual tool for any final touches.

**Limits.** The range of layout complexity has a ceiling — Kodo is well suited to contained formats like social cards, simple posters, and presentation slides, but intricate editorial layouts or anything with tight brand-system constraints will probably need significant rework after export. How much control you have over typography and grid logic once inside the editor is not fully clear from what is published. And if your workflow depends on a specific file format or lives inside a particular ecosystem, check whether the export options actually fit before you commit.
